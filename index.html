About Queen's Rocketry

The Queen’s Rocket Engineering Team (QRET) is a team of students from Queen’s University that share a passion for design and construction of high speed aircraft for the space and aerospace industries. The team builds a rocket each year to compete at the Intercollegiate Rocket Engineering Competition

The objective of the competition is to deliver an 8.8 lb payload to an altitude of 10,000 feet, then track, and recover the rocket and payload in reflyable condition.

Aerodynamics

Primary objectives for the aerodynamics team for the coming year include ensuring static and dynamic flight stability for variable launch conditions, and drag profile estimation for effective deployment of the airbrake mechanism.

Team members will work with a variety of computer programs, including but not limited to ANSYS Fluent, and OpenRocket to provide accurate altitude and flight dynamics predictions.

Electronics

The main goal of the electrical team this year is to increase the redundancy of the payload deployment as well as the parachute deployment systems. These systems should have redundant firing systems, controllers, and altitude/attitude sensors beyond those required by competition rules. This is crucial as in the 2015 competition a lack of redundancy delayed the launch and prevented judges from scoring the team’s rocket. Once landed, a standard radio recovery system will be used, transmitting GPS co-ordinates for rapid, efficient recovery.

Beyond the basic critical elements of the electronics team, there are many more interesting and challenging goals. Feasibility of live telemetry data and low resolution video transmission will be investigated and implemented to whatever extent possible. A HAM radio license will be obtained in order to facilitate testing of sufficiently powerful RF transmitting and receiving equipment.

An auxiliary objective of the electronics team this year will be investigating the feasibility of using structural carbon fiber body components as antennae, to facilitate communications between the rocket and base camp during flight and recovery operations. The complex and noncritical nature of this investigation mean that it will take a secondary role, to be completed to the fullest extent that time and resources allow.

Payload

The payload team for the 2016-17 academic year will focus on development, construction, and testing of the rocket’s 8.8 lb. The payload is expected to consist of an automatic control system to stabilize and control a spinning rocket body falling from apogee.

This payload will also investigate the effects of roll rate on skin friction drag, and will serve as a proof of concept for a stabilization system that can be used for real rocket flight. Additionally, a panoramic camera, and its own standalone tracking and recovery systems will be included.

This subteam will be led by a group of five Engineering Physics students as their 4th year design project, but will require support from a variety of other teams of students.

Structures

The structures team will be responsible for the mechanical design of any load-bearing structures within the rocket, and any of its subsequent subsystems. The team will work heavily in conjunction with the aerodynamics and construction teams in particular, to ensure feasibility of all proposed designs.

The team will revolve heavily around traditional mechanical design, including finite element analysis and topology optimization, using ANSYS Mechanical.

Construction

The construction team is responsible for manufacturing and assembly of all components, from prototyping to the final rocket build. The team will work heavily with testing and developing new composites layup techniques in Queen’s new composites manufacturing facility, and with the Mechanical Engineering Department’s outstanding team of machinists in the machine shop in McLaughlin Hall.

Media

The media team will be responsible for engaging members of the Queen’s and Kingston communities, to provide visibility for STEM fields, academic research, the university, and team sponsors.

Prospective Members

QRET is always actively searching for new members of all experience levels. Queen’s students of any year, faculty, and discipline are encouraged to participate in all aspects of the design process. Please contact us through the website, by email, or on Facebook if you are interested in joining.

As a student design team, QRET acknowledges that its members are, first and foremost, students. As such, it is understood that members’ workload and availability is subject to fluctuation throughout the school year. All members are expected to attend the approximately one hour weekly meetings for their respective subteams. Time requirements beyond this point will be easily scalable, based upon the availability and initiative of each individual.
